📖 What is Diaphragmatic Breathing?▼
The diaphragm is the main muscle used for breathing. When it works well, the abdomen gently moves during each breath. This pattern often requires less effort than chest-dominant breathing. Efficient diaphragm use supports relaxation and steady breathing.
Route
—/10
📖 Why Is Nasal Breathing Important?▼
Breathing through the nose helps filter and warm the air. It also supports smoother airflow at rest. Mouth breathing at rest may increase effort over time. Encouraging nasal breathing can improve breathing efficiency.
CO₂ Tolerance
—/10
📖 What is CO₂ Tolerance?▼
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a natural part of breathing. Your body uses changes in CO₂ to decide when to breathe. CO₂ tolerance reflects how calmly your body responds to these changes. Lower tolerance may lead to quicker or more effortful breathing. Breathing training can gradually improve tolerance over time.
Breathing Recovery
—/10
📖 What Does Breathing Recovery Mean?▼
After activity, your breathing naturally increases. Breathing recovery is the time it takes to return to normal. Faster recovery often reflects better regulation. Delayed recovery may suggest increased system strain.

Single-Leg Heel Raise ASYM

📖 Why Single-Leg Heel Raise?▼
The single-leg heel raise tests calf and ankle endurance — key for propulsion, balance, and fall prevention. Normative means decline with age (~0.25 reps/year after 25). Asymmetry above 20% between sides indicates a lateralised deficit worth addressing.
5× Sit-to-Stand
—/10
📖 Why Sit-to-Stand?▼
The 5× Sit-to-Stand test is a validated measure of lower limb functional power, balance, and transfer ability. Faster times indicate greater strength and neuromuscular efficiency. Age-adjusted norms are applied — expected time increases ~0.06s/year.
Deep Neck Flexor Endurance
—/10
📖 Why Deep Neck Flexors?▼
Deep neck flexor endurance reflects the capacity of the sub-occipital stabilising muscles. These are commonly inhibited in forward head posture and cervical pain. A hold time below 20–25s is clinically significant. Mean is ~30s with a SD of ~10s.
Single-Leg Stance ASYM
—/10
📖 Why Single-Leg Stance?▼
Single-leg stance time measures static balance and proprioceptive integration. Normative means are age-adjusted: ~45s (age <40), ~35s (40–49), ~25s (50–59), ~18s (60+). Values below 10s are associated with increased fall risk. Asymmetry above 20% between sides suggests a lateralised neuromuscular deficit.
